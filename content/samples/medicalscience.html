Sample MCQ

All of the following are criteria for total brain death except:

A. lack of reflexive movements
B. apnea
C. flat electroencephalogram
D. weak pupillary reflex
E. lack of gag reflex

DOKDifficulty
2Easy

Solution

Answer

D

The answer block should contain only the answer identifier (A, B, C, D, etc.) formatted in the same way as the question (lowercase vs. uppercase).
Explanation
  • D: The pupillary reflex is the reaction of the pupils to constrict when light is shone into the eyes. If a person has a pupillary reflex at all, even if it is weak, this indicates brain activity, and they do not meet the criteria for brain death.
Incorrect Explanation(s)
  • A: Reflexes are involuntary; therefore, in a person with typical brain function, they cannot be overridden. For example, when the plantar reflex is tested by stroking the bare sole of the foot, a person with typical brain function responds with reflexive plantar flexion. A person who meets the criteria for brain death will lack reflexes and their foot will not move.
  • B: A person experiencing apnea is one who is not breathing. A person who meets the criteria for brain death is not capable of breathing without assistance.
  • C: An electroencephalogram (EEG) is a measure of a personâ€™s brain waves. A flat, or isoelectric, EEG does not change from the flatline position and is seen in brain death, as it indicates that there is no electrical activity in the brain.
  • E: The gag reflex is an important protective reflex that causes an individual to gag when an object unintentionally enters the pharynx (throat). Lacking this reflex indicates that the brain is not equipped to protect the body against choking and is therefore among the criteria for diagnosing brain death.
Hint(s)

Hint: Total brain death, sometimes just referred to as brain death, is characterized by brain damage that is so extensive that the brain cannot perform necessary functions for the body.

Sample TCQ (TF)

True or False: Holding oneâ€™s breath causes the pH of blood to decrease.

DOKDifficulty
2Easy

Solution

Answer

True

The answer to a TF question can only be "true" or "false".
Explanation

When a person holds their breath, carbon dioxide is not expelled and is retained in their blood. Carbon dioxide has a high affinity for water, forming carbonic acid (H2CO3) when they combine. Formation of carbonic acid within blood causes a decrease in pH (i.e., blood becomes acidic).

At homeostasis, water composes at least 50% of whole blood. Therefore, the longer a person holds their breath, the more carbon dioxide they retain and the more acidic their blood becomes.

When alcohol is consumed, it is metabolized into a toxic substance that enters the bloodstream called __________.

DOKDifficulty
1Easy

Solution

  • acetaldehyde
Explanation(s)

  • Acetaldehyde is generated when an enzyme called alcohol dehydrogenase breaks down consumed ethanol (the type of alcohol found in alcoholic beverages).

Sample SA

Explain the general pathology of Guillain-Barre syndrome.

DOKDifficulty
1Easy

Solution

Sample Response

Guillain-Barre syndrome (GBS) is an autoimmune condition. The immune system is prompted to attack the myelin surrounding the nerves of the peripheral nervous system. As a result, signals from sensory and motor neurons are delayed or stopped. If left untreated, severe cases of GBS can result in significant paralysis.

Explanation

The cause of GBS is still unknown. Many patients with GBS previously experienced some type of infection, though GBS does not occur after every infection. GBS is initially characterized by progressive weakening of the limbs, although the reason is neurological, not muscular. Both the humoral and cell-mediated aspects of the adaptive immune system attack and destroy the myelin sheaths that insulate peripheral nerves.

GBS is not a permanent condition, and its duration is shortened if a person receives intravenous immunotherapy. Physical therapy typically accompanies medical treatment. However, muscle weakness persists in approximately one of three affected individuals.


Hint

Hint: Guillain-Barre syndrome is an acquired autoimmune condition.

Sample ES

When intravenous (IV) fluids are administered to a dehydrated patient, a saline solution is used to prepare the IV drip. Explain why, and describe the consequences of using pure water instead of saline.

DOKDifficulty
3Medium

Solution

Sample Response

As the water in an under-hydrated personâ€™s bloodstream is used up by tissues of the body, the plasma becomes hypertonic (i.e., high ion concentration and proportionately low water concentration) in comparison to the hypotonic red blood cells (i.e., low ion concentration and proportionately high water concentration). Therefore, water is drawn out of the hypotonic red blood cells by osmosis until the cell crenates (shrivels). The ability to deliver oxygen is inhibited in crenated red blood cells. This poses a risk of hypoxemia (i.e., low oxygen concentration in the blood), poor oxygenation of tissue, organ failure, and potentially death. Upon rehydration with intravenous fluids, crenation can usually be reversed, restoring function.

At homeostatic conditions, red blood cells contain a low concentration of ions. Pure water, on the other hand, has an ion concentration of 0%. Because osmosis continues until equilibrium (which could not be reached since ion concentration within the red blood cell cannot be diluted to 0%), pure water would continually move into the red blood cells, causing them to swell and rupture (hemolyze), destroying the cells. This will kill the patient.

Saline is used in IV drips because saline is a hypotonic solution. Thus, water molecules move by osmosis into the red blood cells from the saline solution as it is introduced into the bloodstream. However, equilibrium can be reached between ion concentrations of the red blood cell and the saline, allowing both the plasma and the red blood cells to safely be rehydrated without risk of hemolysis.

Explanation

A state of dynamic equilibrium called homeostasis exists for many facets of the bodyâ€™s chemistry, including the concentrations of its fluids. At homeostasis, plasma (i.e., the fluid component of blood) typically consists of water (approximately 50%), as well as ions and proteins. An under-hydrated personâ€™s blood lacks the proper amount of water, so the formed elements (i.e., the cellular portion of the blood) become proportionately high and the blood becomes more viscous (i.e., thicker) as a result. This risks seizure due to electrolyte imbalance, organ damage or failure, and death.

All solutions, including blood plasma, are made up of solutes (particles that are dissolved such as ions and glucose) and solvents (i.e., components, usually liquid, that dissolve the solutes). Water is considered a universal solvent because its polar nature causes it to dissolve most solutes. Osmosis, the diffusion of water, is driven by the solute concentration of the surrounding environments. For example, when a solutionâ€™s solute concentration is high, solvent is proportionately low. This is referred to as a hypertonic solution. When the opposite is trueâ€”when a solutionâ€™s solute concentration is low and the solvent concentration is proportionately highâ€”this constitutes a hypotonic solution.

During osmosis, water moves from hypotonic solutions to hypertonic solutions until equilibrium is reached. Thus, when IV fluids are given, they are administered directly into the bloodstream of an under-hydrated patient to increase the water concentration within the blood plasma. Increasing the water within the plasma can rehydrate red blood cells to their normal shape and function via osmosis.

Sample ORD

Trace the pathway of blood flow through the heart, starting from the right atrium and ending where blood enters systemic circulation.
a) right ventricle
b) pulmonic valve
c) lungs
d) left ventricle
e) right atrium
f) pulmonary veins
g) aorta
h) tricuspid valve
i) aortic valve
j) pulmonary arteries
k) bicuspid valve
l) pulmonary trunk
m) pulmonary veins

DOKDifficulty
2Medium

Solution

Answer

The correct order is: e, h, a, b, l, j, c, f, m, k, d, i, g

Explanation
  • e: The right atrium is where the blood receives deoxygenated blood returning from the systemic circuit (which supplies blood to the body) via the vena cavae. Deoxygenated blood from coronary circulation (which supplies the cardiac muscle) is also deposited into the right atrium.
  • h: After leaving the right atrium, blood passes through the tricuspid valve. Tricuspid means â€˜three-cusped,â€™ in reference to the three pieces of tissue that compose the valve. The tricuspid valve is sometimes called the right atrioventricular (i.e., separates an atrium from a ventricle) valve.
  • a: Blood from the right atrium drains into the right ventricle. The chambers of the heart can fill with blood when the heart is in a brief state of relaxation between pumps, known as diastole.
  • b: The right ventricle contracts to send blood through the pulmonic valve. This valve is sometimes referred to as the pulmonary semilunar valve in reference to its half-moon shape. Blood is said to be in the pulmonary circuit after passing through this valve until its return to the heart.
  • l: The pulmonary trunk is a large artery that carries deoxygenated blood away from the heart. It splits into a left and right pulmonary artery, which travel to the left and right lung, respectively.
  • j: The pulmonary arteries are among the only arteries that carry deoxygenated blood. They receive blood from the pulmonary trunk and carry it to the lungs.
  • c: The lungs receive deoxygenated blood from the pulmonary arteries. Oxygen is introduced into the blood via the smallest capillaries within the lungs.
  • f: There are two pairs of pulmonary veins: the left and right pulmonary veins, which pick up oxygenated blood from the left and right lungs, respectively, and return it to the heart. The pulmonary veins are among the only veins that carry oxygenated blood.
  • m: Oxygenated blood returns to the left atrium from the pulmonary circuit, which supplies blood to the lungs. The left atrium contracts to eject blood into the next part of the system. The contracted state of any of the heartâ€™s chambers is called systole.
  • k: Blood leaving the left atrium flows through the bicuspid valve. This two-cusped valve is also called the mitral valve or the left atrioventricular valve.
  • d: The left ventricle is the thickest chamber of the heart. because its contraction needs to be strong enough to propel blood into the systemic circuit (i.e., all the vessels of the body).
  • i: When the contraction of the left ventricle generates enough pressure, the aortic valve (also called the aortic semilunar valve) opens. Blood exits the heart and enters systemic circulation through the aortic valve.
  • g: The aorta is the first vessel in systemic circulation. It is a large, elastic artery that is structurally equipped to receive and distribute high-pressure blood ejected from the left ventricle.

Sample DRW

Draw a spirogram of a person at rest exhibiting healthy respiration, including all volumes and capacities. Explain how the spirogram would change in a person experiencing an asthma attack.

DOKDifficulty
2Medium

Solution

Answer
Provide a sample graphic (art asset) and then provide a description of the graphic immediately following the asset. See the Images section for specific instructions on how to generate art assets for solutions. Follow this description with clear instructions directing students how to develop, label, and format their drawings to satisfy the question.


Explanation

There are eight significant volumes and capacities that can be observed from a spirogram. These volumes vary by a personâ€™s height, build, age, and health of their respiratory system.

Total lung capacity is the total amount of air that can be measured in the lung when it is fully inflated.

  • residual volume: This is a small amount of air that always remains in the respiratory passages, keeping the lungs from collapsing. A person cannot exhale their residual volume; it is only removed if the lung is punctured.
  • At rest, the volume of air that is moved with each breath is called the tidal volume.
    • If a person exceeds a normal tidal inhale by taking a deep breath, the volume of air moved is called the inspiratory reserve volume (IRV).
    • If a person exceeds a normal tidal exhale by exhaling forcefully, the volume of air moved is called the expiratory reserve volume (ERV).
      • After the ERV is expelled, residual volume represents the air that remains (i.e., the amount of air that keeps the lungs inflated and cannot be expelled under normal conditions).

    When a person experiences an asthma attack, the respiratory passages constrict. As a result, breaths grow more shallow and the respiratory rate increases.

    • Shallow breaths represent a decrease in tidal volume.
    • Shallower breathing also results in a decrease in both the IRV and ERV because the volumes of inhalations and exhalations are reduced.
    • Because complete exhalations cannot occur during an asthma attack, more air is retained in the lungs, and the residual volume increases.
    • The only volume that remains unchanged is the total lung capacity.
