Sample MCQ

A meteor strikes a satellite orbiting Earth and splits the satellite into two equal pieces. One piece flies off away from Earth, while the other continues to orbit the planet at 50% of its original radius.

What factor describes the change in gravitational force between the Earth and the remaining satellite piece?

A. 2
B. 4
C. 1/8
D. 1

DOKDifficulty
1Easy

Solution

Answer

A

Explanation
  • A: The mass of the piece that remains in orbit is half of the mass. The orbital radius of the piece is also half of the original. Substituting these values into Newton's law of gravitation gives $$F_{g} = \frac{G \frac{m_{1}}{2}m_{2}}{(r/2)^{2}} = \frac{4Gm_{1}m_{2}}{2r^{2}} = 2 \cdot \frac{Gm_{1}m_{2}}{r^{2}}$$Therefore, the gravitational force has changed by a factor of 2.
Incorrect Explanation(s)
  • B: Reducing only the orbital radius by half in Newton's law of gravitation would give a factor of four. $$F_g=\frac{Gm_1m_2}{(r/2)^2}=4\cdot\frac{Gm_1m_2}{r^2}$$
  • C: Doubling the radius instead of reducing it by half in Newton's law of gravitation results in a factor of 1/8. $$F_{g} = \frac{G \frac{m_{1}}{2}m_{2}}{(2r)^{2}} = \frac{Gm_{1}m_{2}}{2 \cdot 4 \; r^{2}} = \frac{Gm_{1}m_{2}}{8r^{2}}$$
  • D: Neglecting to include the change to the radius in r2 in Newton's law of gravitation results in an incorrect factor of 2 in the numerator, leading to a factor of 1 (i.e., no overall change in force). $$F_{g} = \frac{G \frac{m_{1}}{2}m_{2}}{(r)^{2}/2} = \frac{2Gm_{1}m_{2}}{2r^{2}} = \frac{Gm_{1}m_{2}}{r^{2}}$$
Hint(s)

Hint 1: The force is directly proportional to the mass of each object.
Hint 2: The force is inversely proportional to the square of the radius.

C. The image is larger than the object.

DOKDifficulty
2Medium

Solution

Answer(s)

A

D

Explanation(s)
  • A:

    When an object is placed in front of a convex lens at a distance that is greater than the focal length of the lens, the resulting image will be real and inverted. The object is placed at a distance > f, so the image will be real and inverted.
  • D: Use the thin lens equation where f is the focal length, q is the image distance, and p is the object distance.$$\begin{aligned}\frac{1}{f}&=\frac{1}{q}+\frac{1}{p}\\\frac{1}{10\mathrm{\;cm}}&=\frac{1}{q}+\frac{1}{25\mathrm{\;cm}}\\q&=\frac{250\mathrm{\;cm}^{2}}{15\mathrm{\;cm}}=16.7\mathrm{\;cm}\end{aligned}$$The magnification is given by $M = -\frac{q}{p}$, where p is 25 cm and q is the result of the rearrangement of the thins lens equation.$$M = -\frac{16.7 \; \mathrm{cm}}{25 \; \mathrm{cm}} = -0.67$$Since the magnitude of M is less than 1, the image is smaller than the object.
Incorrect Explanation(s)
  • B: A virtual image is produced when the object is placed at a distance < f from the lens. The object here is at a distance > 2f.
    Use the thin lens equation where f is the focal length, q is the image distance, and p is the object distance.$$\begin{aligned}\frac{1}{f}&=\frac{1}{q}+\frac{1}{p}\\\frac{1}{10\mathrm{\;cm}}&=\frac{1}{q}+\frac{1}{25\mathrm{\;cm}}\\q&=\frac{250\mathrm{\;cm}^{2}}{15\mathrm{\;cm}}=16.7\mathrm{\;cm}\end{aligned}$$An image is upright if the magnification given by $M=-\frac{q}{p}$ is positive. Both q and p are positive, so the magnification is negative.
  • C: Use the thin lens equation where f is the focal length, q is the image distance, and p is the object distance.$$\begin{aligned}\frac{1}{f}&=\frac{1}{q}+\frac{1}{p}\\\frac{1}{10\mathrm{\;cm}}&=\frac{1}{q}+\frac{1}{25\mathrm{\;cm}}\\q&=\frac{250\mathrm{\;cm}^{2}}{15\mathrm{\;cm}}=16.7\mathrm{\;cm}\end{aligned}$$The image is larger than the object if the magnitude of the magnification given by $M=-\frac{q}{p}$ is greater than 1.
    Use the values of q and p. $\left|M\right|=\left|-\frac{16.7\mathrm{\;cm}}{25\mathrm{\;cm}}\right|=0.67$.
  • E: Use the thin lens equation where f is the focal length, q is the image distance, and p is the object distance.$$\begin{aligned}\frac{1}{f}&=\frac{1}{q}+\frac{1}{p}\\\frac{1}{10\mathrm{\;cm}}&=\frac{1}{q}+\frac{1}{25\mathrm{\;cm}}\\q&=\frac{250\mathrm{\;cm}^{2}}{15\mathrm{\;cm}}=16.7\mathrm{\;cm}\end{aligned}$$The focal length is 10 cm, so the image is located at a distance between f and 2f.

Sample TCQ (TF)

Samuel drops watermelons at rest from an initial height of 10 m onto two surfaces. The first surface is a concrete floor, and the second surface is a 10 in thick mattress. Samuel claims the watermelon striking the concrete will experience more impulse and more force than the watermelon striking the mattress.

Is Samuel's claim true or false?

DOKDifficulty
1Easy

Solution

Answer

False

Explanation

Only the claim that the object experiences more force is true.

Impulse is defined as the change in momentum of an object and does not depend on time of impact. $$I = \Delta p = m \Delta v$$The object is released from rest in both experiments, so the initial velocity is zero in both cases. The height from which they are dropped is also the same, so they will undergo the same rate of acceleration for the same period of time and strike the surfaces with the same velocity. The impulse is the same regardless which surface the object strikes.

The total force experienced by the object is given by the ratio of the impulse to the impact time.$$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}$$The shorter the time of impact, the greater the force. When the object hits the softer surface, the impact occurs for a longer period of time than the object hitting the harder surface, since the soft surface absorbs the impact. Since the time of impact is shorter when striking the hard surface, the force will be larger.

Hint(s)

Hint: Only one of the two quantities in question (impulse and force) has a dependence on time.

Sample FIB

The buoyant force depends on the _______ of the fluid, the _______ of the object, and _______.

DOKDifficulty
2Medium

Solution

Answer(s)
  • density
  • volume
  • gravity
Explanation(s)

An object submerged in a fluid experiences more pressure on the bottom end than at the top. Because the pressure increases with depth, the pressure gradient buoys the object upward. The pressure on the object at some depth in this fluid is proportional to the density of the fluid that is exerting the pressure, so the buoyant force is also proportional to this quantity.

When the object is submerged in a fluid, it displaces a volume of fluid equal to its own volume, so the buoyant force depends on the object's volume.

The pressure at each point on the object also depends on gravity, so the buoyant force is also dependent on gravity.

Sample ES

Provide a detailed explanation of the ideal gas law and the relationships between each variable. Is there such a thing as an ideal gas?

DOKDifficulty
2Medium

Solution

Sample Response

The equation of state of a gas depends on three primary variables: temperature, volume, and pressure. In order to know something about the condition of any gaseous system, the relationship between these three variables must be established. The ideal gas law is a combination of three empirical laws known as Gay-Lussacâ€™s law, Charlesâ€™s law, and Boyleâ€™s law. These laws established the relationship between the three variables.

Boyleâ€™s law experimentally showed that when the temperature of gas is held constant and the pressure is increased, the volume decreases at an inversely proportional rate, and vice-versa.

$$V \; \alpha \; \frac{1}{P}$$

Charlesâ€™ law experimentally showed that when the pressure of a gas is held constant and the temperature is increased, the volume also increases at a linearly proportional rate.

$$V \; \alpha \; T$$

Guy-Lussacâ€™s law experimentally showed that when the volume of a gas is held constant and the temperature is increased, the pressure also increases at a linearly proportional rate.

$$P \; \alpha \; T$$

Combining the three laws and introducing the constant of proportionality, $nR$, leads to the final expression of the ideal gas law, $PV=nRT$.

Ideal gases are merely hypothetical, and such a gas has never been observed in nature. For a gas to be ideal, every collision between the particles must occur such that no energy is lost in the collision, and this requirement is not physical. Nonetheless, the behavior of many of the gases that are found in the real world can be described by the ideal gas law.

In order to define the ideal gas law:

  • Identify and describe the empirical laws that make up the ideal gas law.
  • Clarify the relationship between individual variables in the gas laws.
  • Explain the gas constant.
  • Define an "ideal" gas.

    Sample ORD

    In what order should the following steps be arranged to describe how to calculate the total amount of required energy needed to heat a 0.5 kg piece of ice at -20°C into steam at 135°C?


    1. Heat to change 0.5 kg of ice into water.
    2. Heat to raise temperature from 100°C to 135°C.
    3. Heat to raise temperature from -20°C to 0°C.
    4. Heat to raise temperature from 0°C to 100°C.
    5. Heat to change 0.5 kg of water into steam.
    DOKDifficulty
    2Medium

    Solution

    Answer

    The correct order is: 3, 1, 4, 5, 2

    Explanation(s)
    • The heat is first used to raise the temperature of the ice from -20°C to 0°C. At this point, the 0.5 kg is still solid ice. $$Q = m_{w}c_{w}(0 \degree C - ( -20 \degree C))$$
    • A phase change must now occur, and the heat goes into changing the 0.5 kg block of ice at 0°C into water at 0°C.
      $Q=mL_f$ where $L_f$ is the latent heat of fusion.
    • There is now 0.5 kg of water at 0°C, so the heat will go toward raising the heat of this water to 100°C.$$Q = m_{w}c_{w}(100 \degree C - 0 \degree C)$$
    • Another phase change occurs, and the heat is used to change the 0.5 kg of liquid water at 100°C into 0.5 kg of steam at 100°C.
      $Q=mL_v$ where $L_v$ is the latent heat of vaporization.
    • In the final step, the heat is used to raise the temperature of the 0.5 kg of steam from 100°C to 135°C.$$Q = m_{w}c_{w}(135 \degree C - 100 \degree C)$$

    Hint: Phase changes are intermediary steps.
