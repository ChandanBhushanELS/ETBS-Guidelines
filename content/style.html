Guiding Principles

Solutions should be concise, informative, and not overly technical. Keep in mind that students are looking for clear, to-the-point solutions that help them better understand the concept being addressed by a specific textbook question.

  • Writing should be succinct and clear. Use bulleted lists, numbered lists, or short paragraphs (no more than 3 to 4 sentences) whenever possible. Avoid large blocks of text and avoid overly complex sentences.
    • Paragraph breaks can be used to separate content when there is no intro sentence and no specific number or hierarchy.
    • Bullets are used when there is an intro sentence and the list is unnumbered.
    • Numbered lists are used when the question asks for a specific number of ideas/concepts or hierarchical answer whether or not there is an intro sentence.
    • If a list item is a structurally complete sentence, it should be capitalized and punctuated as a complete sentence.
    • If any of the items in a list are complete sentences, then all of the items in that same list should be complete sentences.
    • Do not make list items flow into each other. (For example, list items should not end with commas, semicolons, or conjunctions such as "and" or "or.")
  • Examples should be clear, straightforward, and accessible. Examples should enhance a student's understanding of the solution while avoiding overly technical or bloated language.
  • Avoid delving too deeply into teaching a concept in a solution.
  • Each solution should be considered a standalone answer and references to other solutions, specific pages or content in a textbook, or other questions cannot be included.
  • The use of bolding and italics can be used for emphasis, but should be used sparingly. Never bold an entire sentence for emphasis. Never use all caps or quotation marks for emphasis.
  • A spell checking tool or applications should be used before submitting solutions.

Tone and Audience

Writing should be appropriately leveled for college freshmen and sophomores. Write engagingly, but maintain an academic feel. Students should feel like the solution is authoritative, but not dense.

  • Do not use 1st or 2nd person.
  • Avoid using a casual or conversational tone. Avoid personification and editorializing.
  • Avoid the use of unclear references (for example, starting a sentence with "Here" or "This" in reference to a paragraph or sentence above).
  • Avoid including rhetorical questions.

Gender Guidelines

Overall (all subjects except Psychology)

The language in textbook questions is often filled with references to "males" and "females" in such a way that erases trans, intersex, and other individuals who do not fit into that binary. Even if the textbook isn't particularly inclusive with its wording, Evelyn Learning's solutions can attempt to be.

  • Whenever possible, remove references to gender in these solutions. Example: In the sentence "A vaginal infection is a major health concern for adult females that can result in pelvic inflammatory disease," the phrase "for adult females" can be removed without affecting student comprehension.
  • When removing gendered language is not possible, please use the qualifier "cisgender" or "assigned" (e.g., "cisgender women" or "assigned female"). If there is no viable option to reword, leave it as is and flag the copy supervisor in a JIRA comment listing the question code and indicating that a revision is necessary for inclusiveness.
  • Use "they" (not "he/she") as a singular pronoun to refer to an individual whose gender is not specified.
  • Please refer to the subject-specific guidelines for exceptions. Additional subject-specific guidelines will be added as they are developed.

Subject-Specific

Biology and Anatomy/Physiology

  • Avoid phrasing that assigns gender to organs, cells, processes, characteristics, etc. Refer to those organs/cells/processes/characteristics/etc. directly (e.g., "sperm," not "male gamete").
  • If it is absolutely necessary to differentiate between different forms of the reproductive system (for example, if the answer is comparing two different forms of the reproductive system as a whole), use organ-specific terms such as "ovarian reproductive system" and "testicular reproductive system," not "female reproductive system" or "male reproductive system."

Psychology

  • Since Psychology textbooks spend a significant amount of time specifically discussing and deconstructing gender stereotypes, these guidelines cannot be applied to Psychology solutions without altering the meaning of the solution.
  • Specific guidelines for discussing gender in Psychology are under development and will be released in a future update. Please consult the Psychology content lead with any questions.
